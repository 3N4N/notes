## Chapter 2

- This chapter is almost completely Meursault spending a Sunday watching people
  from his balcony.  He did spend the night before with a woman, who was
  surprised by his normalcy after only one day of his mother's passing.  This
  reaction is interesting, but otherwise it was dull and I fail to gather the
  point.

## Chapter 3

- Boss seems relieved after learning Maman was about sixty.  Why?
- Meursault seems like a normal guy.  He enjoys the company of women.  He
  complains to his boss.  He plays childish games with his coworker/friend.
- Salamano and his dog hate each other.  Even tho "they have been inseparable
  for eight years."
- Salamano is heinously abusive to his dog.
- Raymond Sintes.
- Meursault seems to be devoid of conscience.  When asked if he thinks
  Salamano's dog is pitiful, he says no.  When asked if he thinks Sintes abusive
  treatment of his mistress is wrong, he says no.  Is Meursault merely
  indifferent or is there something else going on?

## Chapter 4

- Raymond wants to please Meursault.  When a cop shows up at his door, Raymond
  tries to act cool after noticing Meursault is watching.  Later he asks
  Meursault if he expected Raymond to be more antagonistic toward the police.
- Narratos agrees to give false testimony for Raymond.  It's not being
  indifferent anymore; he is being a criminal now.
- Salamano's dog takes off while he is watching The King of the Escape Artists.
  How's that for irony?
- Even tho he abuses the dog, Salamano is deeply concerned for the dog's
  well-being.  He is also scared of loneliness when the dog isn't with him.

## Chapter 5

- "None of it really mattered."
- When Marie asks Meursault for marriage, he agrees.  "It didn’t make any
  difference to me and that we could if she wanted to."  So Meursault agreeing
  to help Raymond wasn't him being evil; it was still him being indifferent.
- Salamano: "I hope the dogs don't bark tonight.  I always think it's mine."

## Chapter 6

- What the hell is a "drowsy-looking" promontory?
- Why did Meursault shoot the Arab the other four times?  (In ch 7, the
  magistrate asks him the same question.)

## Chapter 7

- Meursault: "I pointed out to him that none of this had anything to do with my
  case."
- "As always, whenever I want to get rid of someone I’m not really listening to,
  I made it appear as if I agreed."  This must be why Meursault agreed to
  Raymond's heinous requests.
- Magistrate: "Monsieur Antichrist."  And that after Meursault explains how he
  feels like "one of the family" in the presence of the magistrate and the
  lawyer.  That's the funniest passage in the book yet.

## Chapter 8

- Too dense.  Need help dissecting it.

## Chapter 9

- Meursault says of the court: "As if they were in a club where people are glad
  to find themselves among others from the same world."
- A reporter explains to Meursault that they have blown up his case a bit, as if
  expecting him to be impressed.  Absurd.
- Meursault: "When I was first imprisoned, the hardest thing was that my
  thoughts were still those of a free man."
- Meursault: "But by then I had gotten used to not smoking and it wasn’t a
  punishment anymore."
- Meursault: "I realized then that a man who had lived only one day could easily
  live for a hundred years in prison. He would have enough memories to keep him
  from being bored."
- Meursault: "I thought the traveler pretty much deserved what he got and that
  you should never play games."

## Chapter 10

- The hearing revolves less around the crime and more around Meursault's
  character.

## Chapter 11

- The last chapter becomes a treatise on philosophy.
